Wetlands

Understanding wetlands and their importance

Definition of Wetlands

A wetland is an area of land covered with surface or groundwater throughout the year or part of the year. Types include marshes, swamps, and bogs.

In Malawi, wetlands include Vwaza, Lake Chilwa, Elephant and Ndindi marshes, Bana and Dzaza swamps, and shallow areas of Lake Malawi.

Wetlands as Ramsar Sites

Ramsar sites are wetlands of international importance designated under the Ramsar Convention.

In Malawi, two Ramsar sites exist: Lake Chilwa and Elephant Marsh.

Flora and Fauna in Malawi Wetlands

Flora

  • Pondweeds
  • Reeds
  • Duckweeds
  • Common water weeds

Fauna

  • Beetles
  • Crane flies
  • Water bugs
  • Snails
  • Snakes
  • Frogs
  • Hippos
  • Fish
  • Crocodiles
  • Mayflies

Importance of Wetlands

  • Habitat for diverse plants and animals.
  • Act as natural sponges, reducing flooding.
  • Purify water by filtering waste and chemicals.
  • Serve as grazing lands for animals.
  • Attract tourists due to scenic beauty.
  • Provide reeds and grasses for domestic use.
  • Support fishing activities.

Human Activities That Threaten Wetlands

  • Draining wetlands for farming and settlement.
  • Damming of rivers.
  • Deforestation causing siltation.
  • Water pollution from poor waste disposal.

Strategies for Managing Wetlands

  • Awareness campaigns on wetland importance.
  • Strong laws for wetland conservation.
  • Proper waste disposal.
  • Tree planting in uplands to reduce erosion.
