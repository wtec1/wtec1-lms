The Tragedy of Macbeth by William Shakespeare

Comprehensive Character Analysis

Macbeth

  • Scottish general who becomes consumed by ambition after hearing the witches' prophecy that he will be king
  • Initially loyal to King Duncan but murders him to seize the throne
  • Experiences severe psychological torment and guilt following Duncan's murder
  • Transforms from noble warrior to paranoid tyrant who orders multiple murders to secure his power
  • Becomes increasingly isolated and detached from his wife as the play progresses
  • Dies in battle against Macduff, fulfilling the witches' prophecy about his downfall
  • Represents the destructive nature of unchecked ambition and moral corruption

Lady Macbeth

  • Macbeth's wife who is initially more ruthless and ambitious than her husband
  • Manipulates Macbeth into murdering Duncan by questioning his manhood and courage
  • Appears strong and calculating in early acts but gradually descends into madness
  • Suffers from guilt-induced sleepwalking and obsessively tries to wash imaginary bloodstains from her hands
  • Dies offstage, presumably by suicide, unable to bear the weight of her crimes
  • Represents the psychological consequences of evil actions and the limits of human willpower

King Duncan

  • Virtuous and trusting King of Scotland
  • Represents legitimate authority and the divine right of kings
  • Murdered by Macbeth while sleeping as a guest in Macbeth's castle
  • His murder violates sacred bonds of kinship, hospitality, and loyalty
  • His death sets in motion the chaos and bloodshed that follows throughout the play

Banquo

  • Macbeth's fellow general and friend who also hears the witches' prophecy
  • Prophesied to father a line of kings though he will not be king himself
  • Unlike Macbeth, resists temptation and maintains his integrity
  • Suspects Macbeth of foul play after Duncan's murder
  • Murdered by assassins hired by Macbeth, who fears Banquo's descendants
  • His ghost haunts Macbeth at the banquet, symbolizing Macbeth's guilt

Macduff

  • Scottish nobleman and Thane of Fife
  • Discovers Duncan's murdered body
  • Becomes suspicious of Macbeth and refuses to attend his coronation
  • Flees to England to join Malcolm and raise an army against Macbeth
  • Learns his wife and children have been slaughtered on Macbeth's orders
  • Kills Macbeth in final battle, fulfilling the prophecy as one "not of woman born" (delivered by caesarean section)
  • Represents loyalty, justice, and righteous vengeance

Malcolm

  • Duncan's eldest son and rightful heir to the Scottish throne
  • Flees to England after his father's murder, fearing for his life
  • Tests Macduff's loyalty by falsely claiming to be unfit to rule
  • Leads the English army against Macbeth to reclaim the throne
  • Becomes King of Scotland at the play's end, restoring legitimate order
  • Represents hope, wisdom, and the restoration of rightful authority

Donalbain

  • Duncan's younger son and Malcolm's brother
  • Flees to Ireland after Duncan's murder
  • His flight makes him temporarily suspect in his father's death
  • Plays a minor role but his escape ensures the royal line's survival

Lady Macduff

  • Macduff's wife who is left defenseless when her husband flees to England
  • Questions her husband's loyalty and wisdom in abandoning his family
  • Murdered along with her children by Macbeth's assassins
  • Represents innocent victims of tyranny
  • Her death motivates Macduff's determination to kill Macbeth

Fleance

  • Banquo's son who escapes when his father is murdered
  • His survival threatens Macbeth's security since the witches prophesied Banquo's descendants would be kings
  • Represents the future and the inevitable fulfillment of prophecy

The Three Witches (Weird Sisters)

  • Supernatural beings who prophesy Macbeth's rise and fall
  • Speak in riddling paradoxes and equivocations
  • Represent fate, temptation, and the dark forces of nature
  • Their prophecies catalyze Macbeth's ambition but do not control his actions
  • Embody the theme of appearance versus reality

Hecate

  • Goddess of witchcraft who supervises the three witches
  • Appears briefly to reprimand the witches for acting without her
  • Represents higher supernatural authority
  • Her scenes may be later additions to the original play

Lennox

  • Scottish nobleman who initially supports Macbeth
  • Gradually becomes suspicious of Macbeth's role in the murders
  • Eventually turns against Macbeth and joins the rebellion
  • Serves as voice of growing discontent among Scottish nobles

Ross

  • Scottish nobleman and Macduff's cousin
  • Acts as messenger throughout the play, delivering important news
  • Initially appears to support Macbeth but eventually joins the rebellion
  • Brings tragic news of Lady Macduff's murder to her husband
  • Represents the political flexibility and survival instinct of nobles during tyranny

Angus

  • Scottish nobleman who turns against Macbeth
  • Joins Malcolm's forces to overthrow the tyrant
  • Represents the nobility's rejection of illegitimate rule

Menteith (Monteith)

  • Scottish nobleman who rebels against Macbeth
  • Joins the forces gathering to restore Malcolm to the throne
  • Minor character representing collective opposition to tyranny

Caithness

  • Scottish nobleman who joins the rebellion against Macbeth
  • Part of the coalition that restores legitimate rule
  • Minor character representing unified resistance

King Edward (Edward the Confessor)

  • King of England who provides sanctuary to Malcolm and Macduff
  • Known for his healing powers and saintly nature
  • Represents legitimate kingship and divine favor
  • Contrasts sharply with Macbeth's tyrannical rule
  • Provides military support to help restore Malcolm to Scottish throne

Young Siward

  • Son of Siward, the Earl of Northumberland
  • Fights bravely against Macbeth's forces
  • Killed in battle by Macbeth
  • His honorable death represents the cost of restoring justice

The Doctor

  • Physician who observes Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking
  • Recognizes that her condition is spiritual rather than physical
  • Represents the limits of medicine in treating guilt and moral corruption
  • Comments on the unnatural events surrounding Macbeth's reign

The Porter

  • Drunken doorkeeper at Macbeth's castle
  • Provides comic relief after Duncan's murder
  • His dark humor about hell and damnation ironically reflects the play's themes
  • Represents the common people and earthly reality amid supernatural drama

The Murderers

  • Hired assassins who carry out Macbeth's orders
  • Kill Banquo and attempt to kill Fleance
  • Murder Lady Macduff and her children
  • Represent the moral degradation under Macbeth's rule
  • Show how tyranny corrupts and employs desperate men

The Old Man

  • Witnessed unnatural events following Duncan's murder
  • Describes omens such as darkness at noon and horses eating each other
  • Represents traditional wisdom and the natural order disrupted by regicide
  • Serves as chorus commenting on the play's supernatural atmosphere
